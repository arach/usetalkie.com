There are more good dictation apps for Mac than there used to be, and they're not really competing for the same job. Some are built for typing by voice into any app. Some transcribe recordings after the fact. One runs your whole computer hands-free. Picking well means matching the tool to what you actually do, not to a feature checklist.

Full disclosure: I make [Talkie](/downloads/), so I'm not a neutral referee. But a roundup that pretends every rival is bad is useless to you and a little embarrassing to write, so I've tried to be honest about when each of these is the better choice — and there are plenty of cases where something here beats Talkie.

## How to think about the choice

Three questions sort most of this out:

1. **On-device or cloud?** On-device tools transcribe on your Mac, so your audio never leaves it — better for privacy and offline use. Cloud tools send audio to a server, which is often what powers their slickest AI formatting. Neither is "right"; it's a trade-off.
2. **Dictation, transcription, or control?** Live *dictation* types your speech into apps as you talk. *Transcription* turns existing recordings into text. *Voice control* drives your whole computer by voice. Different jobs, different tools.
3. **Free, one-time, or subscription?** Prices range from free-forever to professional per-seat licensing.

## Live dictation into any app

**Talkie** is free, on-device, and Apple-only (Mac, iPhone, Apple Watch). What sets it apart is that your captures land in a local database and a CLI exposes them as structured data — so you can dictate straight into agents like Claude Code, not just into text fields. It's the pick if you want private, free dictation that also feeds your tooling. It won't do Windows, big multilingual reformatting, or file transcription.

**[Superwhisper](/ideas/talkie-vs-superwhisper/)** is a mature, powerful app with an extensive catalogue of AI "modes" (email, message, custom) powered by many models, plus meeting recording and 100+ languages. It runs offline on Apple Silicon or in the cloud. Best if you want a big menu of reformatting styles and don't mind paying.

**[Wispr Flow](/ideas/talkie-vs-wispr-flow/)** is the cross-platform polish champion — fast, cloud-based, with excellent auto-editing across Mac, Windows, iPhone, and Android. Best if you work across operating systems and want formatting-first dictation.

**[Aqua Voice](/ideas/talkie-vs-aqua-voice/)** is a fast, cloud AI dictation app with real-time formatting and on-screen context awareness across Mac, Windows, and iOS. Best if low-latency AI cleanup and cross-platform reach are your priorities.

**[DictaFlow](/ideas/talkie-vs-dictaflow/)** is a hybrid (local recognition + cloud reasoning) tool that stands out for typing into locked-down Citrix, RDP, and VMware sessions. Best for Windows-heavy and enterprise remote-desktop workflows.

## Transcribing recordings and files

**[MacWhisper](/ideas/talkie-vs-macwhisper/)** runs Whisper locally on your Mac and excels at audio/video file transcription — batch queues, watch folders, subtitle export, model choice. A one-time price. Best if your job is turning recordings into transcripts.

**[Aiko](/ideas/talkie-vs-aiko/)** is a dead-simple, private, on-device Whisper transcriber for Mac, iPhone, and iPad, at a modest one-time price. Best for straightforward offline file transcription with no fuss.

## Meetings

**[Otter.ai](/ideas/talkie-vs-otter/)** is a cloud meeting-notes service: live transcription, summaries, speaker ID, and team collaboration with CRM and Zoom/Meet/Teams integrations. Best if the job is meetings and shared notes, not personal dictation.

## Built in and hands-free

**[Apple Dictation](/ideas/talkie-vs-apple-dictation/)** is free, built into macOS and iOS, on-device on modern hardware, and honestly fine for quick dictation with zero setup. Best when you just want to talk into a text field occasionally.

**[Talon Voice](/ideas/talkie-vs-talon/)** controls your entire computer by voice — cursor, clicks, commands, and voice coding grammars — and is a cornerstone accessibility tool. Steep learning curve. Best for hands-free operation, RSI recovery, and coding by voice.

**[Dragon](/ideas/talkie-vs-dragon/)** remains the professional command-and-control heavyweight with tuned medical and legal vocabularies — but its consumer Mac product is long gone, so on Mac it's effectively a Windows/enterprise option. Best if you need professional-grade accuracy and control on a supported platform.

If you need Windows, the widest formatting menu, or professional command-and-control, one of the others above is the honest answer.
